# Positioning Angles: Brightline Bookkeeping

> Sample output. Fictional demo brand. This is the kind of menu the mkt-positioning skill hands you: five distinct angles, each with who it lands for and where it is weak, then one clear recommendation. You pick, the skill writes the winner into brand_context/positioning.md.

The job here is not to brainstorm taglines. It is to find the single true thing about this brand that a competitor cannot easily say back. Five candidates, then the pick and why.

---

## Angle 1: The human on a deadline

**The line:** A real human keeps your books clean and closes them by the 10th, every month.

**What it leans on:** The two things founders want most, a competent person and a predictable schedule, in one promise. Most options give you one or the other, never both.

**Lands hardest for:** Founders burned by pooled-team platforms where no one knows their business, and by firms that go quiet for eleven months.

**Where it's weak:** None major. The "by the 10th" claim has to be kept, so it puts a real operational promise on the table. That is a feature, not a bug, but it is a commitment.

---

## Angle 2: Books that pay for themselves

**The line:** We catch the money you're leaving on the table: missed write-offs, double payments, charges you forgot to cancel.

**What it leans on:** Reframes the fee as a thing that returns money, not just a cost. Strong for the skeptical, money-minded founder.

**Lands hardest for:** Founders who think of bookkeeping as pure overhead and need an ROI story to say yes.

**Where it's weak:** Hard to promise honestly for every client. Some founders' books are clean enough that there is little to "catch," and overclaiming here erodes trust fast. Too narrow to carry the whole brand. Better as an ad hook than a foundation.

---

## Angle 3: The anti-software pitch

**The line:** Not another app to babysit. A person who just handles it.

**What it leans on:** The fatigue founders feel toward self-serve tools they signed up for and never finished setting up. Pure positioning against software.

**Lands hardest for:** Founders with three half-configured accounting apps open in tabs they avoid.

**Where it's weak:** Defined by what it is not, which is a weaker place to stand than what it is. Also risks sounding anti-technology, when the real value is the human plus a clean, modern process. Strong as a supporting message, thin as the lead.

---

## Angle 4: Tax season, solved early

**The line:** Walk into tax season with clean, finished books and a calm CPA.

**What it leans on:** The single sharpest pain in the buyer's year. Very easy to feel.

**Lands hardest for:** Founders in their January-to-April panic, which is also peak buying season.

**Where it's weak:** Seasonal. It frames a year-round subscription around a once-a-year event, which can make the value feel like it sleeps for eight months. Risks blurring the line between bookkeeping and tax filing, which the brand works hard to keep clear. Excellent as a Q1 campaign, risky as the core.

---

## Angle 5: Know your numbers, finally

**The line:** Stop guessing whether you made money. Open one summary and know.

**What it leans on:** The emotional core, the not-knowing. Speaks to the quiet anxiety more than the chore.

**Lands hardest for:** Growth-minded founders who want to make decisions on real numbers, not their checking-account balance.

**What's weak:** Closest to the truth of the brand, but on its own it is a feeling without a mechanism. It says what you get, not who delivers it or how reliably. Needs the human and the deadline underneath it to be believable.

---

## Recommendation

**Lead with Angle 1: the human on a deadline.** Fold Angle 5 in as the emotional payoff.

Angle 1 is the only candidate that states both the differentiator (one named human, not a rotating pooled team) and a concrete, ownable promise (closed by the 10th). It is testable, memorable, and honest, and it scales naturally into a monthly subscription because the proof shows up every single month.

The right structure is Angle 1 as the headline and Angle 5 as the reason it matters: a real human closes your books by the 10th (Angle 1), so you open one summary and finally know where you stand (Angle 5). Mechanism plus payoff.

Hold the other three as campaign hooks, not the foundation:

- **Angle 2** goes in the ad-testing matrix as an ROI variant for cost-skeptical buyers.
- **Angle 4** becomes the Q1 tax-season campaign.
- **Angle 3** is a supporting line on the page, not the headline.

This is the angle written into `brand_context/positioning.md` as the primary. The rest live as labeled hooks so nothing good gets lost.
